Beyond the Supporting Role

Black artists have repeatedly transformed American screens. From Hattie McDaniel to Sinners, Black artists have pushed for more than a place on screen.

Podcast episode Listen: Black Artists in Film ↗

By Maya Chelle
A 12-minute read

With the 98th Academy Awards now behind us, Sinners—the acclaimed, genre-defying thriller that made history with a record-breaking 16 nominations—still has everyone talking. From dedicated cinema aficionados to those who only lift an ear in passing when the Oscars roll around.

Note: I am the former. Numerous film references follow.

We have been blessed with many amazing movies over the years. Just last year, the musical adaptation Wicked triumphed on Oscar night. The year before, The Holdovers brought us a masterclass performance with a win for Da’Vine Joy Randolph. In 2022, King Richard earned a historic lead victory for Will Smith, following another powerful win by Daniel Kaluuya for Judas and the Black Messiah one year earlier.

These films all share something that, in previous generations of cinema, was simply not possible: Black artists dominated the screen.

In an ideal world, every win would celebrate the pure talent and dedication of amazing artists indistinguishably. Anyone in the film industry knows that the work comes with harsh challenges and obstacles. Black artists carry the added pressure of historically established discrimination.

Representation has improved, and continues to improve, in cinema. The Academy Awards offer one measure of how slowly that recognition has arrived.

Access, visibility, recognition and power

Looking back at history, one often shudders at the events we have allowed to unfold. For too long, the world of cinema was largely closed off to Black artists.

When Black actors did gain access to Hollywood, they were too often given minor, demeaning or stereotypical roles: the servant, the criminal, the Jezebel, the comic sidekick or the loyal friend who exists mainly to help a white protagonist.

Behind the screen, Black directors, producers and screenwriters struggled to produce or fund their projects. They were set apart under the label “Black cinema.”

Racism within the industry has repeatedly barred the way for their stories.

Representation seemed to mean strategically placing a few Black faces on screen and calling it diverse. Society today knows better. Representation means having a seat in the room—a voice. It means deciding which stories are told and crafting characters that are full, rounded and real.

Cinema represents a mirror of our past, present and future—of our shared humanity. It inspires so many. What appears on screen shapes our knowledge of the past, our perception of the present and our hopes for the future.

One step forward…

By the late 1980s, a new generation of Black filmmakers was forcing Hollywood to widen its frame.

After a decade of unequal representation, with major releases such as Superman, Airplane! and Dressed to Kill built around largely white ensembles, it was time for a change.

Spike Lee’s Do the Right Thing, released in 1989, placed the racial tensions of a Brooklyn neighborhood at the center of the American cinematic conversation. It was followed by an extraordinary period of creative activity. In 1991, with Boyz n the Hood, John Singleton became the first Black filmmaker—and the youngest person—to receive an Academy Award nomination for best director.

Julie Dash’s Daughters of the Dust became the first feature directed by a Black woman to receive a general theatrical release in the United States. Films including New Jack City, House Party, Devil in a Blue Dress and Malcolm X expanded the range of Black stories reaching mainstream audiences.

Television experienced its own boom. A Different World, Living Single, The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air, Martin, Moesha and In Living Color presented Black families, friendships and ambitions with a breadth rarely seen on network television. These programs had richness beyond traditional stereotypes. Each was different; Black life was being portrayed not as a genre, but as life.

In the 1990s, “Black cinema”—or what Hollywood labeled as such—became a defining trend of the decade. Acclaimed director Spike Lee was at the center. Denzel Washington played Don Pedro in Much Ado About Nothing, Will Smith became a Black action hero in Bad Boys, and doors opened.

Yet after the commotion surrounding that moment faded, many Black actors and filmmakers were compartmentalized once again. The performers who had “made it” in mainstream Hollywood were still pushed toward stereotypical roles. Many of the directors, writers and producers who had helped create the boom were left behind.

Julie Dash’s experience showed the limits of the breakthrough. Despite the acclaim for her Daughters of the Dust, she did not direct another theatrical feature for decades. The label “Black cinema” recognized the artistic movement while serving as confinement. Hollywood continued to treat films by Black directors as a specialized category rather than as cinema itself.

Present, but not at the center

Even when Black actors gained access to the biggest levels of the film industry, Hollywood still gave them minor, demeaning and stereotypical parts.

Black performers continued to be cast in roles that were simply there to support the main white actor. The industry was becoming more diverse on screen without necessarily creating what the original debate called a true “Black story”: a story in which Black characters possessed their own depth, choices and point of view.

The stereotypes are familiar: the maid; the jezebel, or hypersexualized woman; the thug; the “sapphire,” a caricature of the angry Black woman; the best friend; and the “magical Negro,” whose special insight exists mainly to rescue or transform a white protagonist. Different stories, different details, same function. The Black character helps complete someone else’s journey.

Movies have also repeatedly diminished the image of Black people through white-congratulatory storylines. In white-savior narratives, racial injustice becomes the setting for a white character’s moral awakening. Films such as The Help, Freedom Writers and The Blind Side brought race to wide audiences, but often placed white benevolence at the emotional center of Black struggle.

The issue is not that Black characters must always be heroic, or that only Black filmmakers may portray Black life. Art requires freedom, contradiction and imagination. The problem arises when a narrow group of gatekeepers repeatedly decides which portrayals are universal, which are marketable and which will never reach the screen.

A 2015 UCLA study illustrated the concentration of that power. Among the 18 major studios it examined, 94 percent of chairpersons and chief executives were white, and all were men. Senior management was 92 percent white.

These are the people deciding which scripts to green-light, which films to fund and whom to cast. If Black artists, who know their communities and their stories, are barred by a mainly white executive class, it is no wonder that the industry returns to familiar stereotypes even while its casts appear more diverse.

Cicely Tyson broke barriers in 1963 as one of the first Black actors to appear regularly in a TV drama, East Side/West Side. An advocate and humanitarian, she built a career portraying strong Black women across film, television and theater. After taking several stereotypical roles early in her career, she began refusing parts that demeaned Black women. This could have cost her career. Through Sounder, The Autobiography of Miss Jane Pittman and Roots, Tyson helped people feel represented and more comfortable in their own skin. Her career became an argument for roles that could better humanity—and the image of the Black community.

Others followed: Denzel Washington, Angela Bassett, Will Smith, Samuel L. Jackson, Whoopi Goldberg and Wesley Snipes—all famous actors who broke through those limits, taking roles that once would have been largely unavailable to them.

Recognition

So what of the Oscars? Even after reaching the highest levels of the industry, recognition was sparse.

In 1940, Hattie McDaniel became the first Black person to win an Oscar, receiving the supporting-actress award for playing Mammy in Gone With the Wind. The achievement was historic, but it also embodied Hollywood’s contradictions: McDaniel was honored for a stereotyped role in a segregated industry, and she was seated separately from her white colleagues during the ceremony.

Twenty-four years passed before Sidney Poitier became the first Black performer to win the Oscar for best actor, for Lilies of the Field. His victory did not open the floodgates. For generations, Black performers and filmmakers remained rare among Oscar nominees and even rarer among winners. They made it, but the gates kept them from the top.

In 2015, activist April Reign captured that frustration in a hashtag: #OscarsSoWhite. Her phrase became a social-justice campaign after the Academy nominated an overwhelmingly white group of actors for its major awards. The campaign addressed precisely this issue, calling out racial and gender inequality in the Academy’s nominations and awards. When the same pattern recurred the next year, the criticism grew into an industry-wide debate about whose work receives prestige, promotion and serious consideration.

Awards are not the only measure of success, but they affect careers in real ways. Salaries, offers, access—recognition helps determine who is seen as an artist worth investing in.

Progress

The racial injustice within film and television is now being addressed more openly. Prominent figures use their influence to inform the public about systemic racism throughout society, including in the entertainment industry. Black actors, directors and producers have been pushing the way forward, creating a space in film for people of color, their cultures and their perspectives.

Spike Lee continued making politically confrontational work long after the industry’s first enthusiasm for the “Black film boom” had passed. Filmmakers such as Ryan Coogler, Barry Jenkins, Regina King and Shaka King expanded the scale and vocabulary of contemporary Black cinema. Shonda Rhimes reshaped network television with shows built around complicated women and multiracial casts.

Performers including Chadwick Boseman, Viola Davis and Daniel Kaluuya portrayed Black characters whose lives were intimate, contradictory and fully their own. Moonlight, Black Panther, Sylvie’s Love, Ma Rainey’s Black Bottom and Judas and the Black Messiah showed that there could be no single Black story—and no single kind of Black audience. They redefined profitability, marketability and what stories are “worth telling.”

Groups such as Astronomy Club have addressed these issues through comedy, in sketches including “Magical Negro Rehab” and “The Audition.” By imagining a rehabilitation program for Black characters exhausted from solving white people’s problems, the group made the stereotype visible. The joke works because the convention is so recognizable.

For those who want to explore the subject further, the documentary series They’ve Gotta Have Us addresses this exact issue. Created by British photographer and filmmaker Simon Frederick, it examines pivotal moments in Black film history that paved the way for African American and Black British artists to achieve mainstream success. Through interviews with actors, producers and directors, the series captures their struggle to move Hollywood’s portrayals of Black people away from simple stereotypes.

Black artists have never lacked stories, talent or ambition. What they have too often lacked is equal access to the system that turns an idea into a production—access to the system that recognizes and monetizes art.

The future of representation depends on the authority to write, direct and finance a broad range of stories.

Sinners—a stereotype on its head

That brings the story back to Sinners. The film subverts nearly every trope that once limited Black characters. Its central figures, Smoke and Stack, are criminals—but they are not stock “thugs.” They are brothers with charm, grief, loyalties, love and moral consequence. Their criminal past enhances their humanity.

Sammie possesses an almost supernatural musical gift, but he is not the old “magical Negro,” placed in the story to save a white protagonist. His music belongs to him, his ancestors and his community. There is no white savior waiting to lead the characters toward freedom. Black people own the juke joint, create the music and drive the action.

They also decide whom to let through the door. Exclusion is turned inside out. The white outsiders are the ones left waiting beyond the threshold of a protected Black space, unable to enter until they are invited. Yet this is not a simple reversal; the film never fails to recognize each character’s depth and humanity. Remmick, the vampires’ Irish leader, carries his own history of displacement. His promise of equality and shared memory sounds compassionate, even as it threatens to absorb individual identity. Added to the hatred, there is a seductive form of assimilation in taking Black music, Black memory and Black community while erasing the people who created them.

Most importantly, Sinners was not a Black face strategically placed inside someone else’s story. Ryan Coogler wrote it, directed it and produced it. Michael B. Jordan carried the screen in two leading roles. Black artists shaped the film’s point of view, and the Academy recognized both the performance and the authorship behind it. Access, visibility, recognition and power.

This article has only scratched the surface. Remember: Black history matters throughout the year—not only in February. Although racial equality in film and television has improved, the fight is nowhere near over.

As Julie Dash observed, many people are still waiting at the gate, even as things move forward.
